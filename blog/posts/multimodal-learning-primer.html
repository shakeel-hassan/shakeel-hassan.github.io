A Primer on Multimodal Learning

November 5, 2024 · 10 min read · ML

Humans effortlessly combine vision, language, sound, and touch to understand the world. We read lips when it's noisy; we match a sound to a scene; we understand a recipe both from the text and the accompanying photo. Machine learning models that can do the same — processing and reasoning across multiple modalities — are called multimodal models.

This post gives a friendly overview of the landscape: what multimodal learning is, why it's hard, and how modern approaches tackle it.

What Counts as a Modality?

Broadly, a modality is a distinct type of input with its own structure and statistical properties. Common modalities in ML include:

  • Images / video — pixel grids with spatial structure
  • Text — sequences of tokens with compositional semantics
  • Audio / speech — continuous waveforms with temporal structure
  • Structured data — tables, graphs, point clouds

Each modality requires specialised processing (CNNs for images, Transformers for text) before we can even begin combining them.

The Core Challenge: Fusion

The central question in multimodal learning is how to combine information from different modalities. There are three broad strategies:

  • Early fusion: Concatenate raw or lightly processed inputs before any deep processing. Simple, but can be overwhelmed by high-dimensional inputs.
  • Late fusion: Process each modality independently to high-level representations, then combine. Modular and easy to train, but misses low-level cross-modal correlations.
  • Intermediate (cross-modal attention) fusion: Exchange information between modality streams at multiple levels. The modern default, enabled by the Transformer's attention mechanism.

CLIP: Contrastive Learning at Scale

OpenAI's CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pre-Training) showed that you could learn powerful image–text representations by training on 400 million image–caption pairs scraped from the web, using a contrastive objective: push embeddings of matching image–text pairs together and mismatching pairs apart.

The result was a model with remarkable zero-shot transfer — you could classify images into arbitrary categories by comparing image embeddings against text embeddings of class names, without any task-specific fine-tuning.

Generative Multimodal Models

More recently, generative models like GPT-4V, Gemini, and LLaVA have extended language models to accept image inputs by:

  1. Encoding images into patch embeddings with a vision encoder (e.g., a ViT).
  2. Projecting these into the LLM's token embedding space.
  3. Letting the LLM attend to both text tokens and image tokens jointly.

This approach is surprisingly effective and scales well — the LLM backbone already knows how to reason; we're just giving it a new type of input to reason over.

Open Problems

Despite rapid progress, multimodal learning still faces several hard problems:

  • Modality imbalance: Models often lean heavily on one modality and ignore the others.
  • Fine-grained grounding: Understanding which part of an image corresponds to which word in a caption remains imperfect.
  • Compositional generalisation: Can a model understand a novel combination of concepts it has only seen separately?
  • Data collection: High-quality aligned multimodal datasets are expensive to curate.

Multimodal learning is one of the fastest-moving areas in ML. I expect the next few years to bring models that process audio, video, text, and structured data with the same fluency that current LLMs handle text alone.
