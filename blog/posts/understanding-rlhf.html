Understanding Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback

February 20, 2025 · 12 min read · ML

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has become one of the most important techniques in modern AI alignment. It is the key ingredient behind ChatGPT, Claude, and most other instruction-following language models. Yet despite its popularity, the mechanics are often poorly understood outside of specialist circles. This post is my attempt to demystify it.

The Problem RLHF Solves

Large language models (LLMs) are pre-trained to predict the next token. This makes them excellent at modelling text distributions, but it does not directly teach them to be helpful, harmless, or honest. A raw language model will happily complete a prompt in a way that is grammatically fluent but factually wrong, unhelpful, or even harmful — because predicting the next token doesn't require caring about those properties.

The goal of alignment is to shift model behaviour from "predicts plausible text" to "behaves in a way humans consider good". RLHF is one way to do that.

The Three-Stage Pipeline

Stage 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Start with a pre-trained LLM and fine-tune it on a dataset of (prompt, ideal response) pairs curated by human labellers. This gives you a baseline model that already follows instructions reasonably well.

Stage 2: Reward Model Training

Generate multiple responses to the same prompt using the SFT model. Ask human annotators to rank them from best to worst. Train a reward model (RM) — typically another LLM with a scalar head — to predict these human preference rankings. The RM distills human judgement into a differentiable signal.

Stage 3: RL Fine-Tuning with PPO

Use Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) to fine-tune the SFT model to maximise the reward model score. A KL-divergence penalty against the original SFT model prevents the policy from collapsing into reward hacking (producing text that scores highly but looks unnatural or degenerate).

The KL penalty is crucial. Without it, the model quickly learns to exploit the reward model's blind spots — a phenomenon known as "reward hacking" or "Goodhart's Law in action".

Open Research Questions

Despite its success, RLHF has well-documented limitations:

  • Reward model over-optimisation: As PPO training progresses, the policy can find inputs that fool the reward model without being genuinely good.
  • Human labeller disagreement: Different annotators have different values; aggregating preferences is non-trivial.
  • Scalable oversight: For tasks where humans can't easily evaluate quality (e.g., long proofs, complex code), we need AI assistance to evaluate AI outputs — an open problem.
  • Distribution shift: The reward model is trained on outputs from the SFT model; as PPO shifts the distribution, the RM may become unreliable.

Alternatives and Extensions

Several recent methods try to sidestep PPO's complexity:

  • DPO (Direct Preference Optimisation): Reformulates RLHF as a supervised learning problem, skipping the explicit reward model entirely.
  • RLAIF: Replaces human annotators with a strong AI model (like GPT-4) to generate preference labels at lower cost.
  • Constitutional AI: Uses a set of principles to guide the model to self-critique and revise its own outputs.
