My Research Workflow: Tools and Habits that Work for Me

January 10, 2025 · 8 min read · Productivity

Research productivity is deeply personal. What works for one person may be completely counterproductive for another. With that caveat in mind, here's what has worked for me after years of trial and error across undergrad, grad school, and industry research.

Paper Management

I use Zotero for reference management. It has a browser extension that lets me save papers with one click, and it integrates with my writing tools. I keep a curated "Reading Queue" collection and try to process it weekly.

For each paper I read seriously, I write a short note (3–5 bullet points) directly in Zotero: the main contribution, the key insight, potential weaknesses, and relevance to my own work. This forces active reading and makes papers recoverable months later.

Code Organisation

Every project gets a repo with a consistent structure: 

project/
  data/          # raw and processed data (gitignored)
  notebooks/     # exploratory Jupyter notebooks
  src/           # library code, importable modules
  experiments/   # runnable scripts, one per experiment
  results/       # outputs, figures (gitignored)
  README.md

I treat notebooks/ as a scratch pad and src/ as production code. When an idea from a notebook proves useful, I refactor it into src/. This keeps the codebase clean without impeding exploration.

Writing

I write papers in LaTeX (Overleaf for collaboration) and use Grammarly for a first-pass grammar check. I find it useful to write the introduction last — after the method and experiments are done, the story becomes much clearer.

For blog posts and longer notes, I use Obsidian. The bidirectional linking feature helps me connect ideas across different projects.

Tracking Experiments

I log all experiments with Weights & Biases. Every run gets tagged with the experiment name and hypothesis. Before running anything, I write down what I expect to happen — this forces me to think clearly and makes surprising results much more obvious.

Weekly Review

Every Friday I spend 30 minutes reviewing the week:

  • What did I actually accomplish?
  • What surprised me?
  • What should I prioritise next week?

This sounds simple, but without it I consistently drifted toward low-priority tasks that felt urgent but weren't important.

Taking Breaks

This is the advice I ignored the longest and wish I hadn't. Scheduled breaks — both within a day (Pomodoro) and across weeks (actual vacations) — make a measurable difference to insight quality. Some of my best ideas have come on walks or while cooking.
